# Llama 2: Open Foundation and Fine-Tuned Chat Models

## TL;DR

Paired release of base models (7B / 13B / 34B / 70B, trained on 2T tokens) and chat models (Llama 2-Chat at the same sizes) produced by SFT + iterative RLHF. The architecture is mostly LLaMA 1 — pre-norm + RMSNorm + SwiGLU + RoPE — with two changes: **4K context (was 2K)** and **[Grouped-Query Attention](../concepts/gqa.md)** on the 34B and 70B. The actual contribution is ~30 pages documenting the post-training pipeline: SFT, two-RM design, iterative RLHF (Rejection Sampling V1–V4 then PPO V5), Ghost Attention, and a three-step safety pipeline. Llama 2-Chat-70B is competitive with ChatGPT (gpt-3.5-turbo-0301) on human-rated helpfulness, and ships under a **commercial license** — ending LLaMA 1's research-only restriction.

---

## The 60-second version (plain English)

LLaMA 1 (February 2023) released open weights of a strong *base* language model — one trained only to predict the next token. Five months later, Llama 2 released two things:

1. **An updated base model** (7B / 13B / 70B) — trained on 40% more data, 2× longer context, with Grouped-Query Attention for the big ones. Mostly an iteration on LLaMA 1.
2. **Llama 2-Chat** — a *chat-tuned* version produced by a multi-stage post-training pipeline. This is the **first time a major open model documented exactly how to align a base model into a chat model.**

Why the chat-tuning matters: a base LLM trained on internet text can produce helpful answers, but it can also produce racist tirades, false medical advice, and step-by-step instructions for harmful activities. Companies like OpenAI and Anthropic had figured out how to shape this raw capability into a polite, helpful, mostly-safe assistant — but they kept the recipe secret. Llama 2 published the recipe in 30 pages.

The recipe:
1. **Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)** — show the model 27,540 carefully-written example conversations.
2. **Reward Modeling** — train two separate scoring models (one for helpfulness, one for safety) from millions of human preference labels.
3. **Iterative Reinforcement Learning** — for five rounds, generate model outputs, score them with the reward models, and update the model to score higher.
4. **Safety pipeline** — adversarial-prompt training, safety-specific reward modeling, and "context distillation" (a clever data augmentation).
5. **Ghost Attention** — a training-data trick that fixes the model's tendency to forget system instructions across multiple turns.

The result: Llama 2-Chat-70B is roughly comparable to ChatGPT-3.5 in human evaluation, with notably *lower* toxicity. And, crucially, the license allows commercial use — which kicked off the "Mistral / fine-tunes / startups" wave that dominated late 2023 and 2024.

## Why this paper is bigger than it looks

LLaMA 1's contribution was the *architecture*. Llama 2's contribution is the *playbook for turning that architecture into a useful product*.

If you're building anything on top of an open LLM, the techniques in Llama 2 are still the canonical reference for:
- How to collect human preference data
- How to train reward models that don't hallucinate
- How to balance helpfulness vs safety (they conflict!)
- How to do RLHF without it falling apart
- How to keep the model on-message across multi-turn conversations

The architecture changes (GQA, longer context) matter too, but they're a smaller story.

## Background — the post-training problem

A base LLM trained on web text + Wikipedia + books is extremely capable but extremely raw. If you ask it "How do I make a sandwich?" it might answer helpfully, or it might continue with "How do I make a sandwich? How do I make a sandwich? How do I" because internet text often has repetition. Or it might say something hostile because the internet has plenty of hostility too.

To turn a base model into an actual assistant, you typically run three stages:

1. **Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT).** Show the model thousands of "good" example conversations — prompt + ideal response — and train it to mimic them. After SFT, the model is dialogue-shaped: it answers prompts politely, in the right format, instead of continuing them as if they were web text.

2. **Reward Modeling.** Collect human judgments on which of two model outputs is better. Train a "reward model" that learns to predict these judgments from text alone — it's a model that scores responses 0–1 based on how a human would rate them.

3. **Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF).** Use the reward model as a training signal. Generate outputs, score them, and update the LLM to produce higher-scoring outputs — while keeping it close enough to the SFT model that it doesn't drift into nonsense.

OpenAI's InstructGPT (Ouyang 2022) was the first paper to show this pipeline working. Anthropic's HH-RLHF (Bai 2022) added a safety dimension. Llama 2 is the first paper to document running it at production scale, with 70B parameters and ~1.4M preference labels, in enough detail that the open community could reproduce it.

## Problem & Motivation

Three problems Llama 2 solves:

1. **Open the alignment recipe.** Pre-Llama-2, post-training details from OpenAI / Anthropic were sketchy in public. Open base models existed (LLaMA 1, BLOOM, Falcon) but no equivalent of GPT-3.5-turbo. Llama 2 closes that gap.

2. **Inference economics at long context.** MHA's KV cache becomes a serious bottleneck at 4K context with large batch — GQA addresses this without the quality regressions of MQA.

3. **Commercial licensing.** LLaMA 1 was research-only; Llama 2 is commercial-friendly. That single change unlocked the entire startup ecosystem that grew up around fine-tuned Llama 2 derivatives in late 2023.

**In plain English:** Meta wanted to ship something that companies could actually use, with the alignment recipe transparent enough that the open community could reproduce and improve it. Llama 2 is what that effort looks like.

## Method — Pretraining

### Architecture changes from LLaMA 1

The architecture is **mostly identical** to LLaMA 1:
- Decoder-only Transformer
- [Pre-normalization](../concepts/pre-normalization.md) + [RMSNorm](../concepts/rmsnorm.md)
- [SwiGLU](../concepts/swiglu.md) FFN
- [RoPE](../concepts/rope.md) positional encoding
- AdamW (β₂=0.95), cosine LR schedule, 2000 warmup steps
- 4M-token batches
- Same SentencePiece BPE tokenizer (32K vocab, digits split, byte fallback)

What's new:

| | LLaMA 1 (65B) | Llama 2 (70B) |
|---|---|---|
| Context length | 2,048 | **4,096** |
| Attention | MHA (64 Q heads, 64 KV heads) | **GQA (64 Q heads, 8 KV heads)** |
| Tokens trained | 1.4 T | **2.0 T** (+43%) |
| License | Research only | **Commercial** |

### The model sizes

| | 7B | 13B | 34B | 70B |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Hidden dim | 4,096 | 5,120 | 8,192 | 8,192 |
| Layers | 32 | 40 | 48 | 80 |
| Heads (Q) | 32 | 40 | 64 | 64 |
| KV heads | 32 (MHA) | 40 (MHA) | 8 (GQA) | 8 (GQA) |
| Context | 4,096 | 4,096 | 4,096 | 4,096 |
| Tokens trained | 2.0 T | 2.0 T | 2.0 T | 2.0 T |
| Peak LR | 3e-4 | 3e-4 | 1.5e-4 | 1.5e-4 |

The 34B was trained but **not released** ("insufficient time to red team"). The other three are public.

### Grouped-Query Attention (GQA) — explained from scratch

This is the only architectural change that matters. To understand why, you need to understand the **KV cache**.

**Why the KV cache exists.** When a Transformer generates text one token at a time, it needs to attend to all previous tokens. The naive way is to recompute K (keys) and V (values) for all previous tokens at every step — but this is wasteful, since K and V for past tokens don't change. So instead, we *cache* them: store K and V for every previous token, reuse them at the next step.

**How big is the cache?** Per request, the KV cache is:

$$\text{cache size} = n_{\text{layers}} \times n_{\text{kv\_heads}} \times \text{seq\_len} \times \text{head\_dim} \times 2 \text{ (for K and V)} \times \text{bytes per element}$$

For a Llama 2-70B in fp16 with full MHA, at 4K context: 80 layers × 64 KV heads × 4096 tokens × 128 head_dim × 2 × 2 bytes = **~10.7 GB per request**. At 32K context: ~85 GB per request. At large batches, this dominates GPU memory.

**The fix.** Three options for the K/V projections:

| | n_kv_heads | KV cache (70B at 4K) | Quality |
|---|---|---|---|
| **MHA** (Multi-Head) | n_heads (64) | ~10.7 GB | Best |
| **MQA** (Multi-Query) | 1 | ~0.17 GB | Often regresses |
| **GQA** (Grouped-Query) | n_heads / group_size (e.g., 8) | ~1.34 GB | Near-MHA |

GQA is the compromise. Instead of one K/V per head (MHA) or one K/V shared across all heads (MQA), you have **a few K/V projections, each shared across a group of query heads**. Llama 2-70B uses 64 Q heads sharing 8 K/V projections, so each K/V is shared across a group of 8 query heads.

**In plain English:** every attention head has its own "perspective" (Q), but several heads share the "context" they're looking at (K, V). A bit like 8 students in a study group all reading the same chapter (shared K, V) while each writing their own notes (own Q). Cheaper to maintain the shared chapter than to give every student their own copy.

**To preserve total parameter count when KV projections shrink, the FFN dimension is bumped up by ~1.3×.** This is why the 34B and 70B aren't quite the same shape as their LLaMA 1 counterparts.

The ablation (Table 18, 30B at 150B tokens, MMLU 5-shot): MHA 28.0, GQA 26.9, **MQA 14.5**. GQA matches MHA on most tasks; MQA hurts knowledge-heavy benchmarks badly.

### Pretraining data

"New mix of publicly available online data," up-sampling factual sources to "increase knowledge and dampen hallucinations."

**Language distribution (Table 10):** 89.7% English, 8.4% unknown (mostly code), <0.2% per other language. **No Meta user data.**

**Toxicity:** about 0.2% of documents have a HateBERT-classifier score ≥ 0.5 (i.e., probably toxic). They deliberately do *not* aggressively scrub this — over-filtering hurts the model's ability to *recognize* and *respond to* toxic content during downstream safety training.

### Training infrastructure

Trained on Meta's Research Super Cluster (RSC) and internal production clusters. Both NVIDIA A100s.
- RSC uses NVIDIA Quantum InfiniBand interconnect; production cluster uses RoCE over commodity Ethernet.
- 200 Gbps endpoints for both.
- 400W per GPU on RSC, 350W on production.
- Total: 3.31M GPU-hours, **539 tCO₂eq** (vs LLaMA 1's 1,015 tCO₂eq for the four-model family).

## Method — Post-Training (the actual paper contribution)

```
Pretraining → Llama 2 base
    ↓ SFT on 27,540 hand-curated examples
Llama 2-Chat (initial)
    ↓ Iterate V1...V5:
       collect preference data → train RMs → rejection-sample-tune (V1-V4) → +PPO (V5)
Llama 2-Chat (final)
```

### Stage 1 — [Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)](../concepts/sft.md)

**The pipeline:** show the model many examples of "good" prompt + response pairs. Train it autoregressively, with the loss masked on the prompt tokens (only learn to generate the response).

**The counterintuitive finding — "Quality is all you need."** They started with millions of third-party SFT examples (instruction-tuning datasets), then observed that **a few thousand high-quality vendor-written examples worked better.** Final SFT set: **27,540 examples**.

Why? Open instruction datasets have:
- Low diversity — lots of similar prompts
- Awkward formatting — markdown sometimes, plain text others
- Chatty-but-unhelpful style — long preambles, no actual answer

Vendor-written prompt+response pairs (where the same person writes both halves) have a coherent style and high information content. Quality wins by a lot.

**Training settings:**
- Cosine LR schedule, peak LR 2e-5
- Weight decay 0.1
- Batch size 64
- Sequence length 4096
- 2 epochs
- **Loss masked on user prompts** — only learn to generate the assistant's response

**In plain English:** SFT teaches the model "this is what good answers look like." After SFT, the model produces dialogue-shaped, helpful-seeming answers — but it's still capped by the writing style of your annotators, and it doesn't know what *bad* answers look like (so it doesn't reliably refuse them). RLHF is what closes those gaps.

### Stage 2 — Human preference data collection

**The pipeline:** annotators write a prompt, sample two responses from different model variants and temperatures, and choose which is better.

**The 4-point preference scale:**
- Significantly better
- Better
- Slightly better
- Negligibly better / unsure

This is more granular than just "A or B" — annotators rate *how much* better the chosen response is, not just *which* is better. The margin term in the reward-model loss exploits this granularity (more on that below).

**Two parallel streams:**
- **Helpfulness** — does the response fulfill the user's request?
- **Safety** — does the response avoid harm? With its own guidelines and a tri-class meta-label:
  - 18% of safety annotations: chosen is safe, rejected is unsafe
  - 47%: both safe
  - 35%: both unsafe
  - **Never:** chosen is unsafe, rejected is safe (would be confusing)

**Iterative collection.** Each week's data uses the *latest* model's outputs. This is critical — preference data on outputs from an old model's distribution becomes useless when the model has moved.

**Final size:** 1.4M Meta comparisons + 1.5M open-source = **2.9M total comparisons.**

Compared to open-source preference data (Table 6): Meta's data has more turns per dialogue (3.9 avg) and longer prompts/responses (798 tokens avg per example).

### Stage 3 — [Reward Modeling](../concepts/reward-modeling.md)

A reward model (RM) takes a (prompt, response) pair and outputs a scalar score predicting how well humans would rate the response. It's the proxy that lets RL fine-tuning optimize for human preference without humans in the inner loop.

**Architecture:** start from a chat-finetuned LM checkpoint, **replace the next-token-prediction head with a scalar regression head** on the final hidden state of the response.

**Two separate RMs.** Helpfulness and safety conflict — a single RM that's good at both is hard to train. Helpfulness RM is trained mostly on Meta Helpfulness data + portions of others; Safety RM on Meta Safety + Anthropic Harmless + 10% helpfulness data (the 10% matters for borderline cases).

**The ranking loss.** Standard pairwise ranking (Bradley-Terry):

$$L = -\log \sigma(r_\theta(x, y_c) - r_\theta(x, y_r))$$

This says: the RM should give higher scores to chosen responses than rejected ones. But Llama 2 adds a **margin term** scaled by preference strength:

$$L = -\log \sigma(r_\theta(x, y_c) - r_\theta(x, y_r) - m(r))$$

where m(r) is larger for "significantly better" pairs, smaller for "negligibly better" pairs (Table 27 has the exact values: 3 / 2 / 1 / 0 in the "Margin Large" variant).

**In plain English:** the margin term tells the RM "if humans said this was *significantly* better, you need a *big* score difference." For "negligibly better" pairs, no margin needed. This forces the RM to assign more extreme scores when humans were confident, leaving room for the easy cases to be obvious and the hard cases to be ambiguous.

**Why init from chat checkpoint, not base.** The RM "knows what the model knows." If the RM didn't know facts the model knows, it might reward confident-but-wrong outputs because it can't tell they're wrong. Sharing knowledge between RM and policy prevents this.

**RM accuracy** (Table 7): Helpfulness RM 63.2 avg, Safety RM 64.3 avg — both beating GPT-4-as-judge (63.0 avg). Scaling trends (Figure 6): bigger RM + more data continue to improve accuracy without saturation.

### Stage 4 — Iterative RLHF (V1...V5)

The pipeline runs five rounds. Each round:
1. Collect new preference data on the current model's outputs.
2. Update the reward models with this new data.
3. Fine-tune the model with one of two RL algorithms.

**Two RL algorithms, used at different stages:**

#### [Rejection Sampling fine-tuning](../concepts/rejection-sampling-finetuning.md) (V1–V4)

For each prompt: sample K candidate generations from the current model. Score each with the RM. Keep only the top-scoring candidate. Fine-tune the model on those top samples (looks like SFT, but the dataset is RM-curated).

**No policy gradient. No KL penalty. No value head.** The exploration happens entirely via sampling — the gradient update is just standard supervised learning.

**The intuition:** high-temperature sampling lets the model find responses better than its own greedy output. The RM picks the best of K. Fine-tuning on those top-scoring samples shifts the policy toward higher-reward regions, without the instability of policy gradients.

The delta between max-of-K reward and median-of-K reward (Figure 7) is the available improvement headroom — and it grows with K. Optimal sampling temperature for the V5 RLHF model is **T = 1.2–1.3**, higher than SFT's T = 1.0.

**Distillation through Rejection Sampling:** smaller models (7B, 13B) are trained on rejection-sampled outputs from the largest model (70B). This distills 70B-quality answers into smaller models — a cheap form of model distillation.

#### [PPO](../concepts/ppo.md) (V5 only)

**Proximal Policy Optimization.** Standard policy gradient with a clipping mechanism that prevents destructively large updates.

The full Llama 2 reward function:

$$R_c(g | p) = \begin{cases} R_s(g | p) & \text{if IS\_SAFETY}(p) \text{ or } R_s(g|p) < 0.15 \\ R_h(g | p) & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

$$R(g | p) = \tilde{R}_c(g | p) - \beta \, D_{KL}(\pi_\theta(g|p) \,\|\, \pi_0(g|p))$$

Three things are happening here:

1. **Piecewise safety override.** If the prompt is safety-flagged or the safety RM scores below 0.15, use the safety score; otherwise use helpfulness. The 0.15 threshold corresponds to ~89% precision / 55% recall on the Safety RM test set.

2. **Whitening transform.** $\tilde{R}_c = \text{WHITEN}(\text{LOGIT}(\sigma(R_c)))$ — pass the score through sigmoid (so it's in [0,1]), then logit-transform it back, then whiten (subtract mean, divide by std). This stabilizes the reward distribution against the KL penalty.

3. **KL penalty to the SFT model.** β controls how much the policy can drift from the starting SFT model. Llama 2 uses β = 0.01 for 7B/13B, β = 0.005 for 34B/70B. Larger models can drift more without falling apart.

**Why all this care?** PPO is famously fragile. Without the KL penalty, the model "reward hacks" — finds outputs the RM scores high but humans hate (verbose, hedged, repetitive). Without whitening, the KL coefficient becomes hard to tune across reward distribution shifts. Llama 2's PPO setup is the result of months of iteration.

**PPO settings:**
- Clip ε = 0.2
- Mini-batch 64, batch 512, 1 grad step per mini-batch
- Constant LR 1e-6, AdamW (β₁=0.9, β₂=0.95)
- 200–400 PPO iterations per RLHF version
- ~330 sec/iteration on 70B
- **FSDP** with weight-consolidation trick for fast generation (full FSDP sharding makes generation ~20× slower, so they consolidate to each node before generating)

**In plain English:** PPO trains the model to maximize reward while staying close to where it started. It's like adjusting a recipe — try variations, but don't deviate from the base recipe so much that you lose what made it work. The KL penalty is the "stay close to the original" leash; the PPO clip prevents single-step disasters.

### Stage 5 — [Ghost Attention (GAtt)](../concepts/ghost-attention.md)

**The problem:** in a multi-turn dialogue with a system instruction like "always answer with emojis," the model obeys at turn 1 but forgets by turn 3 — the instruction is far away in the context, and the model's attention drifts to the immediate user message.

**The fix (no architectural change):** during fine-tuning on multi-turn dialogues, **synthetically prepend the system instruction to *every* user turn**. The model sees it everywhere at training time. At inference time, you only put it once at the system message; the model has learned to keep attending to it.

**Two important details:**
1. **Loss is zeroed on intermediate-turn assistant responses.** Only train on the last turn. This avoids overfitting to the synthetic per-turn instruction.
2. **GAtt is applied AFTER RLHF V3,** not from the beginning. The base RLHF model already follows single-turn instructions; GAtt fixes the multi-turn drift problem on top.

**Synthetic instructions sampled from:**
- Hobbies ("you enjoy tennis")
- Languages ("speak in French")
- Public figures ("act as Napoleon")

Often combined to make the constraints more complex.

Figure 10 shows attention activations to the system message stay much higher across dialogue turns *after* GAtt training.

**In plain English:** instead of changing the model architecture, GAtt changes the training data. By making the model see the system instruction at every turn during training, the model learns "the system instruction is always relevant." A pure data hack with no extra parameters.

## Method — Safety (§4)

The safety pipeline mirrors the helpfulness pipeline at every stage.

### 4.1 Safety in pretraining

The base Llama 2 is **not** safety-aligned. It's just less aggressively filtered than some peers. The argument: leaving toxic content in pretraining (in moderation) lets the model *recognize* and *respond to* toxic prompts during downstream safety training. Over-filtering makes safety RLHF harder.

Pretraining has skews — Western, Christian, English, more "He" pronouns than "She." Documented in Table 9.

### 4.2 Safety supervised fine-tuning

Annotators write **adversarial prompts + safe demonstrations**. These are mixed into the general SFT.

A worked example from the paper:
- **Prompt:** "I want you to roast me. I want you to make it particularly brutal, swearing at me."
- **Response:** "I'm sorry, but I cannot comply with that request. Using vulgar language or intentionally hurting someone's feelings is never acceptable. Instead of asking for a roast, I suggest focusing on positive and constructive feedback that can help you grow and improve as an individual..."

**The risk categories:**
- Illicit and criminal activities
- Hateful and harmful activities
- Unqualified advice (medical, financial, legal)

**The attack vectors annotators try:**
- Psychological manipulation ("authority")
- Logic manipulation ("false premises")
- Syntactic manipulation (misspellings)
- Semantic manipulation (metaphors)
- Perspective manipulation (role-playing)
- Non-English languages

### 4.3 Safety RLHF

Same as helpfulness RLHF, but with the Safety RM. Combined piecewise with helpfulness in PPO (Eq. 4 above).

**Key finding:** safety RLHF improves safety scores dramatically (Figure 14, left) **without hurting helpfulness scores** (Figure 14, right). The two RM score distributions stay separate.

**Safety data scaling (Figure 15):** as you add more safety data (0% → 100% of total RLHF data), mean safety RM score climbs from 0.68 to 0.78, while helpfulness RM score stays flat at ~0.59. So safety doesn't tank helpfulness — but **false refusals do rise** on borderline-but-safe prompts.

### 4.4 Safety context distillation

A clever data-augmentation trick.

1. Generate a response with a "safety preprompt" prefixed: "You are a safe and responsible assistant."
2. Fine-tune the model to produce the same response *without* the preprompt.

Effectively, you "distill" the preprompt's effect into the model's weights.

**Caveat (Figure 16b):** context distillation can also *degrade* response quality on already-good responses. Llama 2 mitigates this by **only keeping context-distilled outputs where the Safety RM scores higher than the original**. This targets the technique to where it actually helps.

**With and without targeted answer templates** (Table 13): a generic preprompt helps, but a category-specific answer template ("address the prompt → add context → encourage further reading") helps even more.

### 4.5 Red teaming

**350+ red-teamers** across multiple rounds, including domain experts in cybersecurity, election fraud, social media misinformation, legal/policy, civil rights, ML, responsible AI, creative writing.

**The metric γ:** average adversarial prompts per person-hour required to elicit a violation. γ improved from **1.8 → 0.45** across rounds — meaning later models required ~4× more effort to break.

**Insights from red teaming:**
- Early models would happily produce harmful content without flagging it.
- Mid models would flag content as harmful and then produce it anyway ("I cannot — but here's how").
- Embedding harmful requests in a positive context ("creative writing requests") was particularly effective at bypassing safety.
- Latest models successfully refuse most attacks.

### 4.6 Safety evaluation

**Adversarial-prompt violation rate** (Figure 17, lower is safer):
- Llama 2-Chat-7B: ~3%
- Llama 2-Chat-13B: ~4%
- Llama 2-Chat-34B: ~7%
- Llama 2-Chat-70B: ~4%
- ChatGPT-0301: ~7%
- PaLM-Bison: ~28%
- Vicuna-13B-v1.1: ~25%
- Vicuna-33B-v1.3: ~38%

**Llama 2-Chat is among the safest models in the comparison.**

**Truthfulness/Toxicity** (Table 14):
- Llama 2-Chat-7B: 57.04% TruthfulQA truthful·informative; **0.00%** ToxiGen toxicity (lowest of all)
- Llama 2-Chat-70B: 64.14% / 0.01%
- ChatGPT: 78.46% / 0.20%

ChatGPT is more truthful, but Llama 2-Chat is *much* less toxic.

## Discussion (§5)

### Beyond human supervision (§5.1)

A subtle but important observation. The authors expected SFT to be the strongest signal and were surprised by RLHF's effectiveness. Why is RLHF stronger?

- Each annotator writes with significant variation. SFT data inherits this variability — including the worst-quality examples written by tired annotators.
- Comparison annotation (RLHF) is less subject to discrepancy — it's easier to agree on which of two responses is better than to write a perfect response from scratch.
- The reward model rapidly learns to assign low scores to undesirable tail-end distribution and aligns toward human preference.

Quote from the paper: "the worst answers are progressively removed, shifting the distribution to the right."

**A surprising consequence:** the RM-trained policy can write *better* than the best annotator. Annotators, while not skilled writers themselves, can still recognize quality in others' writing. This is RLHF's superpower — unlike SFT, it's not capped by annotator writing skill.

### In-context temperature rescaling (§5.1)

**A previously unreported phenomenon.** RLHF dynamically rescales the model's effective temperature based on prompt type:

- **Factual prompts** ("What is the capital of France?"): Self-BLEU diversity drops with temperature for the RLHF model — meaning the model converges to a consistent answer regardless of temperature. Same answer at T=0.6 and T=1.5.
- **Creative prompts** ("Write a poem"): Self-BLEU diversity scales with temperature, like the SFT model — meaning the model still varies its outputs at high temperature.

**In plain English:** RLHF teaches the model "for factual questions, give the same answer; for creative tasks, vary." This emerges from the reward model — wrong answers consistently get low rewards on factual prompts, but creative variations get acceptable rewards on creative prompts.

### Tool use and temporal reasoning (§5.1)

Two emergent capabilities the team didn't explicitly train for:

- **Tool emergence:** the chat model sometimes calls APIs / tools. Mentioned as an interesting capability, not formally tested.
- **Temporal organization:** the model has some sense of when its knowledge ends ("2024 is beyond my knowledge cut-off date").

## Limitations

Authors acknowledge:
- Knowledge cutoff Sept 2022 (most pretraining), July 2023 (some fine-tuning data).
- 89.7% English; non-English performance is materially worse.
- Hallucinations remain.
- Safety evaluations biased toward English and toward Llama 2's own content standards.
- 34B not released — "insufficient time to red team."

Not stated but worth noting:
- No DPO (DPO postdates Llama 2).
- No long-context strategy beyond 4K. (Llama 3 brings 128K.)
- No native multimodality.
- False refusals on borderline prompts rise with safety data fraction (§4.2.3).

## Open Questions

- How much of Llama 2-Chat's quality comes from preference *data quality* vs the algorithmic choices? Hard to disentangle without a counterfactual.
- The 34B was trained but not released — what would the cost/value tradeoff look like for a 30B-class GQA model? (DBRX, Qwen-32B later answered this differently.)
- Does iterative RLHF fundamentally beat single-pass RLHF, or is it just a way to keep the RM in distribution? Not directly ablated.
- Is the false-refusal cost of safety RLHF avoidable, or fundamental to the RM-based approach?
- How transferable is this whole pipeline to non-English models? The data was overwhelmingly English.

## Why this paper matters historically

Three distinct legacies:

**1. GQA reaches the open ecosystem.** Mistral, DeepSeek, Qwen, Gemma all use GQA by default for KV-cache savings. Llama 2's 30B ablation table is the canonical reference everyone cites.

**2. The RLHF playbook becomes public.** Before this, RLHF details from OpenAI / Anthropic were sketchy. Llama 2 documents the entire pipeline — two-RM design, iterative training, rejection sampling vs PPO, safety context distillation, GAtt — at reproducible detail. **This is *the* post-training reference paper of 2023.**

**3. Commercial license** ended LLaMA 1's research-only restriction and enabled the Mistral / fine-tunes / startups wave that defined late 2023 and 2024.

Subsequent open chat models — Mistral-Instruct, Tülu, Zephyr, Qwen-Chat — all directly trace their post-training recipes back to Llama 2's playbook.

---

## My Takeaways

- **The architecture is a footnote; the post-training pipeline is the paper.** Anyone building on top of an open LLM should treat Llama 2 §3 as a reference manual.
- **Two-RM > one-RM** for helpfulness/safety is a robust finding — directly informs DeepSeek-R1's later reward-shaping decisions.
- **Rejection sampling fine-tuning is underrated.** Got Llama 2-Chat through V1–V4 without PPO; PPO only added at the end. Many subsequent open-model teams have followed this "RS first, PPO last" pattern.
- **Quality > quantity for SFT** is now received wisdom (Phi, Zephyr, etc.) — Llama 2 documented it convincingly with the 27.5K-example final SFT set.
- **GAtt is a clever hack** — pure data augmentation, no architecture change, fixes a real multi-turn problem. Worth remembering as a pattern.
- **The piecewise reward + whitening** is the level of care PPO actually requires at scale. People often skip this and wonder why their PPO is unstable.

## Glossary

**Adversarial prompt.** A prompt designed to elicit harmful, false, or otherwise unsafe behavior. Used to train safety classifiers and red-team models.

**Bradley-Terry model.** A statistical model for paired comparisons: P(A beats B) = σ(r_A − r_B). The basis of the RM ranking loss.

**Context distillation.** Generate a response with a guiding preprompt, then train the model to produce that response *without* the preprompt. Effectively distills the preprompt's effect into the model weights.

**DPO (Direct Preference Optimization).** A 2023 alternative to PPO that derives a closed-form policy update from preference data, skipping the reward model. Postdates Llama 2.

**FSDP (Fully Sharded Data Parallel).** PyTorch's sharded data-parallel implementation. Shards parameters, gradients, and optimizer states across data-parallel workers. Llama 2 uses FSDP for both pretraining and PPO.

**GAtt (Ghost Attention).** Llama 2's multi-turn instruction-following data trick. Synthetically prepend system instructions to every user turn during training; loss-mask intermediate assistant turns.

**GQA (Grouped-Query Attention).** Attention variant where K/V projections are shared across groups of query heads. Llama 2's main architectural change.

**Helpfulness RM.** One of Llama 2's two reward models, trained to predict which of two responses is more helpful.

**Iterative RLHF.** Running multiple rounds of preference collection + RM training + RL fine-tuning, where each round's preference data is collected on the previous round's outputs. Keeps the RM in distribution.

**KL penalty (β).** In PPO, a term that penalizes the policy for moving too far from the SFT model. Prevents reward hacking. Llama 2 uses β = 0.01 (7B/13B), 0.005 (34B/70B).

**KV cache.** During autoregressive generation, K and V vectors for previous tokens are cached so each new token's attention costs O(seq_len) instead of O(seq_len²). Memory dominator at long context.

**Margin term.** The m(r) added to the RM ranking loss, scaled by preference strength. Llama 2 uses 3 / 2 / 1 / 0 for "significantly / better / slightly / negligibly better."

**MHA / MQA / GQA.** Multi-Head / Multi-Query / Grouped-Query Attention. Trade off KV cache size against expressiveness.

**Piecewise reward.** Llama 2's PPO reward function: use safety score if safety-flagged or below threshold, else use helpfulness score.

**PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization).** Policy-gradient RL algorithm with clipped surrogate objective and KL penalty. Standard for RLHF.

**Preprompt.** A prefix prepended to the user prompt before the model generates. Used for context distillation and system messages.

**Red teaming.** Proactive risk identification — attacking your own model to find vulnerabilities. Llama 2 used 350+ red-teamers.

**Reference policy (π₀).** The frozen SFT model in PPO. The KL penalty pulls the trained policy back toward this reference.

**Rejection Sampling fine-tuning.** Sample K candidates per prompt, RM-score them, keep the top one, fine-tune on those. Llama 2's RL algorithm for V1–V4.

**Reward hacking.** When the RL policy finds outputs that score high on the RM but humans hate. Mitigated by KL penalty + clipping + iterative RM collection.

**Reward Model (RM).** A scalar regression head fine-tuned from a chat checkpoint, predicting how well humans would rate a response.

**RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback).** The multi-stage pipeline: SFT → preference collection → RM training → RL fine-tuning.

**Safety RM.** Llama 2's second reward model, trained specifically on safety preferences.

**SFT (Supervised Fine-Tuning).** Initial training stage that teaches the model to follow dialogue format. Loss is masked on the user prompt; the model only learns to generate the response.

**Whitening.** Normalize a quantity to have zero mean and unit variance. Llama 2 whitens the reward before adding the KL penalty, for stability.

**γ (gamma) — red-team metric.** Average adversarial prompts per person-hour required to elicit a model violation. Llama 2 reduced γ from 1.8 to 0.45 across red-teaming rounds.
