# LLaMA: Open and Efficient Foundation Language Models

## TL;DR

LLaMA is a family of decoder-only Transformer language models (7B / 13B / 33B / 65B) trained by Meta on 1–1.4T tokens of *only public* data. By training smaller models past Chinchilla-optimal token counts, LLaMA-13B beats GPT-3 (175B) on most benchmarks and LLaMA-65B competes with Chinchilla-70B and PaLM-540B. The weights were released to the research community — this paper kicked off the modern open-weight LLM era and locked in the architectural recipe that almost every subsequent open LLM uses (pre-norm + RMSNorm + SwiGLU + RoPE).

---

## The 60-second version (plain English)

Big language models like ChatGPT are trained to predict the next word in a sentence. Train one on enough text and it becomes good at writing essays, answering questions, and translating languages.

In early 2023, the best language models were *closed* — GPT-3 (OpenAI), Chinchilla and PaLM (Google). Researchers couldn't see the weights or the training data. A few open alternatives existed (BLOOM, OPT, GPT-NeoX) but they were considerably worse.

**LLaMA from Meta changed that.** It's four open models (7B, 13B, 33B, 65B parameters). Two surprising claims:

1. The 13-billion-parameter model **beats GPT-3 (175B)** on most benchmarks — despite being 13× smaller.
2. The recipe uses *only publicly available data* — no proprietary corpora.

How did they pull this off? Two ideas:

- **Train smaller models on far more data.** Earlier scaling laws ("Chinchilla") said use ~20 tokens per parameter for compute-optimal training. LLaMA pushed past this — the 7B model was trained on ~143 tokens per parameter. The result is no longer training-compute-optimal, but it's much cheaper to *run* (which is what matters in production).
- **Use only public datasets** so the model could be released openly. They built a curated mix of CommonCrawl, Wikipedia, GitHub code, books, ArXiv papers, and StackExchange.

The model architecture is mostly borrowed pieces from earlier papers (GPT-3, PaLM, GPT-NeoX). What's novel is the *combination* and the commitment to long training. That recipe became the standard for nearly every open LLM that came after.

LLaMA 1 is the spark. The whole open-LLM ecosystem of 2023–2024 traces back to it.

## What you need to know to read this paper

A handful of background concepts make everything else clearer.

**Transformer.** A type of neural network from 2017 that processes sequences. Has two halves originally — encoder (reads input) and decoder (writes output) — but modern LLMs use only the decoder. The decoder reads a sequence and predicts the next token, then the next, then the next. ChatGPT, Claude, and LLaMA all work this way.

**Token.** The unit the model works with. Sometimes a whole word ("cat"), sometimes a subword piece ("ing"), sometimes punctuation. LLaMA's tokenizer has a vocabulary of 32,000 different tokens.

**Parameters.** The numbers inside the neural network that get adjusted during training. A "65B parameter model" has 65 billion of them. More parameters = more capacity to memorize and generalize, but also more compute to train and run.

**Pretraining.** Training the model to predict the next token, on a giant pile of text. This gives the model its broad knowledge. *Fine-tuning* (later) specializes it for tasks like dialogue.

**Compute.** Measured in FLOPs (floating-point operations) or GPU-hours. Training a 65B model takes roughly a million GPU-hours. The cost is a few million dollars at typical cloud rates.

**Inference.** Using the model after training — answering one prompt at a time. Inference cost is paid forever per query, while training is paid once. This distinction is central to LLaMA's design.

**Attention.** The Transformer's defining mechanism. For each token, the model computes how relevant every other token is to it (via "queries" and "keys"), then takes a weighted combination of their "values." This is what lets it relate "she" to "the doctor" 20 words earlier.

## Problem & Motivation

**The headline problem:** before LLaMA, the best open LLMs (BLOOM, OPT, GPT-NeoX) were noticeably worse than the best closed ones (GPT-3, Chinchilla, PaLM). This held back research because:

1. **You can't study a model you can't run.** Researchers couldn't probe how GPT-3 thinks; they could only call its API.
2. **You can't build on what you don't have.** Every group needed to retrain from scratch.
3. **You can't audit what you can't see.** Closed models' biases and failure modes were hard to investigate.

**In plain English:** science requires reproducibility. AI research had become a black box. LLaMA was Meta's argument that an open model could be as good as a closed one if you trained it the right way.

### The deeper insight: rethinking what "optimal" means

The dominant scaling-laws paper at the time was **Chinchilla** (Hoffmann 2022). It argued: for a fixed *training-compute budget*, the best LLM uses roughly equal scaling of parameters and tokens — about **20 tokens per parameter**. So a 70B model should be trained on ~1.4T tokens. Earlier work (Kaplan 2020) had suggested scaling parameters faster than data, which made models like GPT-3 (175B params, only ~300B tokens) significantly under-trained.

LLaMA's authors observed something Chinchilla missed: **training compute is paid once, but inference compute is paid forever per query**. If you're running the model in production, you'd rather have a small model trained for a long time than a large model trained for a shorter time — even if the smaller model is no longer "compute-optimal" by Chinchilla's definition.

So they trained:
- **LLaMA-7B on 1T tokens** = ~143 tokens per parameter (≈7× over Chinchilla optimum)
- **LLaMA-13B on 1T tokens** = ~77 tokens per parameter (≈4× over)
- **LLaMA-33B on 1.4T tokens** = ~42 tokens per parameter (≈2× over)
- **LLaMA-65B on 1.4T tokens** = ~22 tokens per parameter (right around Chinchilla's recommendation)

Crucially, they explicitly note: "we find that the performance of a 7B model continues to improve even after 1T tokens." The smaller models still hadn't finished learning.

**In plain English:** if you're going to use a model a billion times, it's worth spending more compute up-front to make it small. A 7B model trained for a long time is cheaper to serve than a 70B model that's slightly smarter — and the gap is often small enough that the 7B wins on cost-quality.

### The constraint: only public data

GPT-3, Chinchilla, and PaLM all trained on undocumented or proprietary data ("Books – 2TB," "social media conversations," etc.). LLaMA committed to using *only* publicly available datasets. This was a precondition for releasing the weights.

## Background / Prior Work

A non-exhaustive list of what LLaMA's authors built on:

- **The Transformer** (Vaswani 2017) — the underlying architecture.
- **GPT-2 / GPT-3** (Radford 2019, Brown 2020) — established that scaling language modeling produces general-purpose few-shot learners.
- **Kaplan scaling laws** (Kaplan 2020) — earliest guidance on how to allocate parameters vs data.
- **Chinchilla** (Hoffmann 2022) — refined Kaplan; showed compute-optimal allocation favors smaller models on more data.
- **PaLM** (Chowdhery 2022) — closed Google 540B model; LLaMA's authors borrowed PaLM's SwiGLU activation and benchmark suite.
- **GPT-NeoX** (Black 2022) — open 20B model; LLaMA borrowed its rotary positional embeddings (RoPE).
- **OPT, BLOOM, GLM** — earlier open-weight models that LLaMA outperforms.
- **CCNet** (Wenzel 2020) — the data-cleaning pipeline LLaMA uses on CommonCrawl.
- **xformers / FlashAttention** (Dao 2022) — memory-efficient attention kernels used during training.

---

## Key Contributions

1. **State-of-the-art among open-weight LLMs at release.** LLaMA-65B is competitive with Chinchilla-70B and PaLM-540B; LLaMA-13B beats GPT-3.
2. **Demonstrated that public-only data is sufficient.** This was not previously established at this scale.
3. **Established the canonical decoder-only architecture stack** (pre-norm + RMSNorm + SwiGLU + RoPE). Almost every later open LLM uses it.
4. **Released weights to research community**, seeding the open-LLM ecosystem.

**In plain English:** the paper's lasting impact is less the specific numbers (which were quickly surpassed) and more the *recipe* — a small set of architectural choices that became the default for everyone who came after.

## Method

### Pretraining data — 1.4 trillion tokens, all public (Table 1)

| Source | Sampling | Epochs | Disk |
|---|---|---|---|
| English CommonCrawl (CCNet pipeline) | 67.0% | 1.10 | 3.3 TB |
| C4 | 15.0% | 1.06 | 783 GB |
| GitHub | 4.5% | 0.64 | 328 GB |
| Wikipedia (20 European langs) | 4.5% | 2.45 | 83 GB |
| Books (Gutenberg + Books3) | 4.5% | 2.23 | 85 GB |
| ArXiv | 2.5% | 1.06 | 92 GB |
| StackExchange | 2.0% | 1.03 | 78 GB |

**In plain English:** the model sees a giant pile of text (about 1.4 trillion word-pieces) drawn from seven sources. The biggest source is the public web crawl. Wikipedia and books are seen roughly twice each because they're high-quality. Code from GitHub teaches programming. ArXiv teaches scientific reasoning. StackExchange teaches Q&A format.

#### How CommonCrawl is filtered (CCNet)

Raw CommonCrawl is mostly garbage — boilerplate, duplicates, machine-translated text, low-quality content. The CCNet pipeline cleans it in stages:

1. **Line-level deduplication** — remove identical lines repeated across documents (header/footer text, navigation menus).
2. **Language identification** with fastText — keep only English pages.
3. **n-gram language model filter** — train a small language model on Wikipedia, score every page; drop pages with high perplexity (low quality).
4. **Quality classifier** — train a fastText classifier to discriminate "pages used as references in Wikipedia" from random pages. Filter out the random-looking ones.

The final result is ~3.3 TB of relatively clean English text. This is the bulk of LLaMA's training material.

**In plain English:** they teach a small classifier "what does Wikipedia-grade text look like?" then use that classifier to throw away the worst of the web.

#### How GitHub is filtered

Only repositories with permissive licenses (Apache, BSD, MIT) are kept — important for legal redistribution. Then heuristics filter low-quality files (long alphanumeric strings = likely binary, very short files = likely incomplete). Then file-level exact-match deduplication.

#### Tokenization

LLaMA uses **byte-pair encoding (BPE)** via SentencePiece. Two notable choices:

- **Numbers split into individual digits.** "1234" becomes ["1", "2", "3", "4"]. Helps the model do arithmetic by treating each digit positionally.
- **Bytes-fallback for unknown UTF-8.** Any character not in the vocab is decomposed into raw bytes — no `<unk>` token. Means the model can handle anything Unicode throws at it.

Vocabulary size: **32,000 tokens.**

**In plain English:** the tokenizer is a dictionary mapping pieces of text to integers. LLaMA's dictionary has 32,000 entries. The "split numbers into digits" choice means math problems like "what is 23 + 45" are tokenized as `["w", "h", "a", "t", " ", "i", "s", " ", "2", "3", " ", "+", " ", "4", "5"]` — each digit is its own token, which makes arithmetic learnable.

### Architecture — Transformer with three modifications

LLaMA is a **decoder-only Transformer** (same architectural family as GPT-2/3) with three specific upgrades. Each upgrade was published as a separate paper before LLaMA; LLaMA's contribution was combining them into a recipe that scales.

#### Modification 1 — Pre-normalization with RMSNorm

In the original Transformer, every sub-layer (attention, FFN) was followed by a LayerNorm:

```
x' = LayerNorm(x + Sublayer(x))                # post-LN, original
```

GPT-3 and LLaMA use **pre-LN** instead, where the norm is applied to the *input* of the sub-layer:

```
x' = x + Sublayer(LayerNorm(x))                # pre-LN, modern
```

**In plain English:** this small change moves the normalization from the *output* of each sub-layer to its *input*. It sounds trivial but it dramatically improves training stability for deep models.

The reason: in post-LN, gradients have to pass *through* a normalization at every block on their way back to earlier layers. The norm squashes them. In pre-LN, the residual stream stays clean — gradients flow back through it without being squashed. Deep networks become trainable.

The norm itself is **RMSNorm** rather than LayerNorm:

$$\text{LayerNorm}(x) = \gamma \cdot \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma} + \beta$$

$$\text{RMSNorm}(x) = \gamma \cdot \frac{x}{\sqrt{\text{mean}(x^2) + \varepsilon}}$$

RMSNorm drops the mean-subtraction and the bias term β, keeping only the rescaling by root-mean-square. **In plain English:** LayerNorm centers and rescales. RMSNorm only rescales. The mean-centering turns out not to do much in practice, so dropping it saves compute without hurting quality.

For a hidden dim of 4096, RMSNorm has 4096 parameters (the γ scale vector); LayerNorm has 8192 (γ + β).

#### Modification 2 — SwiGLU activation

The feedforward layer (FFN) inside each Transformer block originally looked like:

```
FFN(x) = ReLU(x · W_1) · W_2
```

Two matrix multiplications with a ReLU activation in the middle. PaLM and LLaMA replace this with **SwiGLU** — a *gated* activation:

$$\text{FFN}(x) = \bigl(\text{Swish}(x W_1) \odot (x W_3)\bigr) W_2$$

where:
- $\text{Swish}(z) = z \cdot \sigma(z)$ (a smooth, non-monotonic activation)
- $\odot$ is elementwise multiplication
- $W_1$ and $W_3$ are two *separate* learned input projections
- $W_2$ is the output projection

**In plain English:** instead of one input matrix and an activation, SwiGLU has *two* input matrices. One branch (`Swish(xW_1)`) is the activation; the other branch (`xW_3`) is a "gate" that modulates the first elementwise. The model can choose to suppress (multiply by ~0) or amplify (multiply by ~1) each dimension of the activation.

It's empirically stronger than ReLU. Why? The gate gives the FFN more expressive capacity per parameter — it's roughly equivalent to giving the network multiplicative interactions in addition to additive ones.

**Parameter math:** a vanilla 4d-hidden ReLU FFN has parameters `2 · d · 4d = 8d²` (input + output). A SwiGLU FFN at 4d-hidden would have `3 · d · 4d = 12d²` (input gate + input value + output). To keep the parameter count comparable, LLaMA uses hidden dim **(2/3) · 4d ≈ 2.67d**:

$$\text{params}(SwiGLU) = 3 \cdot d \cdot \tfrac{8d}{3} = 8d^2 = \text{params}(ReLU\,4d)$$

In practice the hidden dim is rounded up to a multiple of 256 (kernel-friendly). For LLaMA-7B (`d=4096`), the FFN hidden dim is **11,008** — close to (2/3)·4·4096 = 10,922, rounded up.

#### Modification 3 — Rotary Positional Embeddings (RoPE)

A Transformer is permutation-invariant by default — it doesn't know which token came first. Original Transformers fixed this by adding sinusoidal positional embeddings to the input. LLaMA uses **RoPE** instead.

**The math:** pair up the head dimension into 2D blocks. For position `m`, rotate each block by an angle $m \cdot \theta_i$, where $\theta_i = 10000^{-2(i-1)/d}$ — a geometric series of frequencies, one per block.

Concretely, for a 2D pair `(x_1, x_2)` at position `m`:

$$\begin{pmatrix} x_1' \\ x_2' \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \cos(m\theta_i) & -\sin(m\theta_i) \\ \sin(m\theta_i) & \cos(m\theta_i) \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{pmatrix}$$

Applied to the query and key vectors at every attention layer (not the values).

**Why this works:** the dot product `q_m · k_n` after rotation equals a function of `(m - n)` only — the absolute positions cancel out. So attention naturally becomes relative-position-aware, without any learned position embeddings or relative-position biases.

**In plain English:** instead of tagging each token with "I'm at position 5," RoPE rotates the query/key vectors by an angle that depends on position. When you compare two tokens, the angles partially cancel — and what's left depends only on how far apart they are. So the model can tell "5 tokens ago" from "100 tokens ago" without ever learning explicit position embeddings.

Bonus benefit: RoPE generalizes to longer contexts more gracefully than learned position embeddings, because the rotation extrapolates naturally.

#### Putting it all together — a LLaMA decoder block

```python
class LLaMABlock(nn.Module):
    def forward(self, x, freqs_cis):
        # Pre-norm + attention + residual
        x = x + self.attn(self.attn_norm(x), freqs_cis)
        # Pre-norm + FFN + residual
        x = x + self.ffn(self.ffn_norm(x))
        return x
```

Inside `attn`: standard multi-head attention, with RoPE applied to Q and K before the dot product. Inside `ffn`: SwiGLU. Both norms are RMSNorm.

This is the entire architectural delta from the 2017 Transformer. It's stunning how little changed — and how much that small change mattered.

Notes:
- **Head dimension is constant at 128** across all sizes. Only the *number* of heads grows with hidden dim.

- **Batch size is 4 million tokens** per optimizer step for all models.

### Optimizer

- **AdamW** (Loshchilov & Hutter 2017), β₁ = 0.9, **β₂ = 0.95**.
- The lower-than-typical β₂ helps stability at scale by making the variance estimate respond faster to changes in gradient magnitude.
- **Cosine learning-rate schedule** decaying to 10% of peak.
- Weight decay 0.1, gradient clipping 1.0, **2000 warmup steps**.

**In plain English:** AdamW is the default optimizer for Transformers. The cosine schedule starts the learning rate high and gradually slows it as training progresses — like easing off the gas as you approach a destination. The 2000 warmup steps gradually ramp the learning rate up at the very beginning, when the model's gradients are most chaotic.

### Efficient implementation (the part that's invisible at the model-definition level)

Several optimizations made the multi-month training run feasible:

**Memory-efficient causal attention** (xformers library / Rabe & Staats 2021).
- Standard attention computes the full `Q · K^T` matrix (sequence × sequence) and stores it for the backward pass. At 2K context, that's 4M attention scores per head.
- The optimized version never materializes this matrix. It re-computes attention in a streaming fashion, with much less memory.
- Also: Q·K computation is *skipped* for masked positions (the upper triangle in causal attention). Half the work goes away.

**Manual transformer-layer backward pass.**
- PyTorch's autograd computes gradients automatically but stores all intermediate activations. For deep Transformers, this uses too much memory.
- LLaMA implements the backward pass manually for transformer layers, with **selective activation checkpointing** — they save the expensive linear-output activations and recompute the cheap ones (norms, residuals) during backward.
- Tradeoff: more compute per step, less memory.

**Sequence + tensor parallelism** (Korthikanti 2022).
- The 65B model doesn't fit on one GPU. Parameters are split across many GPUs.
- *Tensor parallelism*: split each weight matrix across GPUs (each GPU does part of the matmul).
- *Sequence parallelism*: at the boundaries, split along the sequence dimension instead of repeating activations across GPUs.

**Compute-communication overlap.**
- After each parallel layer, GPUs need to synchronize via `all_reduce`. This is slow.
- LLaMA's implementation overlaps the next layer's compute with the previous layer's all-reduce, hiding the communication cost.

**Result:** ~380 tokens/sec/GPU on **2,048 NVIDIA A100-80GB GPUs**. Training the 65B on 1.4T tokens took **~21 days** of wall-clock time.

**In plain English:** the model itself is small in code. What makes training a 65B model possible is the engineering around the model — clever memory tricks, slicing the model across thousands of GPUs, and overlapping computation with communication. Without these, a single training run would take a year instead of three weeks.

## Experiments

20 benchmarks across 7 categories, evaluated zero-shot or few-shot (1, 5, 32, 64). Compared against GPT-3 (175B), Gopher (280B), Chinchilla (70B), PaLM (8B / 62B / 540B), and the open-weights peers OPT, GPT-J, GPT-NeoX.

### Common-sense reasoning (Table 3, zero-shot)

8 benchmarks: BoolQ, PIQA, SIQA, HellaSwag, WinoGrande, ARC-easy, ARC-challenge, OpenBookQA. These test things like physical intuition ("if a glass falls off a table, what happens?"), social reasoning, and basic factual reasoning.

**Headline results:**
- **LLaMA-65B beats Chinchilla-70B on 7 of 8 benchmarks** (loses on BoolQ).
- **LLaMA-65B beats PaLM-540B on most benchmarks** (loses on BoolQ and WinoGrande).
- **LLaMA-13B beats GPT-3 (175B) on most benchmarks**, despite being 13× smaller.

### Closed-book QA (Tables 4, 5)

Natural Questions and TriviaQA. The model is asked a factual question with no retrieval — it has to know the answer from pretraining alone.

- **LLaMA-65B is SOTA in zero/few-shot.** TriviaQA 5-shot: 72.6%. Natural Questions 5-shot: 35.0%.
- **LLaMA-13B runs on a single V100** for inference and is competitive with GPT-3 / Chinchilla — that single fact made the open-LLM ecosystem suddenly viable for hobbyists and small teams.

### Reading comprehension (Table 6)

RACE benchmark (reading comprehension exams designed for Chinese high schoolers, in English).

- LLaMA-65B competitive with PaLM-540B on RACE-high; LLaMA-13B beats GPT-3.

### Math reasoning (Table 7)

MATH (12K middle/high-school problems in LaTeX) and GSM8k (grade-school word problems).

- **LLaMA-65B beats Minerva-62B on GSM8K** (50.9% vs 52.4% with majority voting boost) — *despite Minerva being a math-specialized PaLM finetune*.
- Still well below Minerva-540B.

### Code generation (Table 8)

HumanEval (164 hand-written programming problems) and MBPP (974 problems).

- **LLaMA-65B beats LaMDA-137B and PaLM-540B** on both. HumanEval pass@1: **23.7%**.
- This without any code-specific training. (Code-specialized variants like Code LLaMA came later, beating these by 2-3×.)

### Massive Multitask Language Understanding (Table 9, 5-shot)

MMLU: 57 subjects covering humanities, STEM, social sciences. The closest thing to a "knowledge test" the field has.

- LLaMA-65B: **63.4** average.
- Chinchilla-70B: **67.5**.
- PaLM-540B: **69.3**.

**This is LLaMA's most honest weakness.** Authors hypothesize the cause: their pretraining contains only ~177 GB of books and academic papers, vs. up to 2 TB in Gopher / Chinchilla / PaLM corpora. **High-quality factual text matters more for MMLU than for most other benchmarks.**

### Brief instruction-tuning experiment (§4)

Following Chung 2022's protocol, they fine-tune LLaMA-65B on instruction-following data → produces "LLaMA-I" → MMLU jumps from **63.4 → 68.9** (matching Chinchilla-70B and beating other instruction-tuned moderate-sized models).

This is flagged as exploratory — the paper isn't really about instruction tuning. But the result foreshadows Llama 2-Chat and the entire RLHF wave that followed.

**In plain English:** instruction tuning is a brief follow-up training step that teaches the model to follow instructions like "summarize this" or "translate to French." A few thousand examples are usually enough. Even a tiny instruction-tuning run improves base-model benchmarks meaningfully.

## Bias, toxicity, and truthfulness (§5)

Pretraining mostly on web data carries the web's biases and toxicity. The paper measures this honestly.

### RealToxicityPrompts (Table 11)

100K prompts; the model completes them; an external classifier scores toxicity (0 = clean, 1 = toxic). Two prompt types:

| Model | Basic prompt | "Respectful" prompt |
|---|---|---|
| LLaMA-7B | 0.106 | 0.081 |
| LLaMA-13B | 0.104 | 0.095 |
| LLaMA-33B | 0.107 | 0.087 |
| LLaMA-65B | **0.128** | **0.141** |

**Toxicity *increases* with model size**, and adding "respectful" framing — which helps small models — actually *hurts* the 65B. This is concerning: it suggests larger models internalize toxicity patterns more thoroughly, and the standard mitigations stop working.

**In plain English:** bigger model = more toxic, surprisingly. Asking the model politely backfires at the largest size. Llama 2 had to address this directly with safety fine-tuning.

### CrowS-Pairs (Table 12)

Measures stereotype reproduction across 9 categories (gender, religion, race, etc.). Higher score = more bias.

- LLaMA-65B average: **66.6** (vs GPT-3: 67.2, OPT: 69.5). Slightly *less* biased on average.
- Worse than peers on **religion** (+10% over OPT), age, and gender.

### WinoGender (Table 13)

Coreference resolution biased toward occupational gender stereotypes. Sentences like "The nurse notified the patient that his shift would be ending in an hour" — does "his" refer to the nurse or the patient?

- LLaMA shows clear gender-occupation correlation bias, especially on "gotcha" cases where the pronoun *doesn't* match the stereotypical gender.
- Performance drops substantially on these adversarial cases — indicating the model is using the occupation's stereotypical gender to do coreference, not the actual sentence semantics.

### TruthfulQA (Table 14)

Measures truthfulness on questions designed to elicit common false beliefs.

- LLaMA-65B: **57%** truthful, **53%** truthful·informative.
- GPT-3-175B: 28% / 25%.
- Better than GPT-3 by a lot, but still hallucinating frequently.

## Carbon footprint (§6, Table 15)

Training a 65B model is energy-intensive.

- LLaMA-7B: **14 tCO₂eq**, 36 MWh, 82,432 GPU-hours
- LLaMA-13B: **23 tCO₂eq**, 59 MWh, 135,168 GPU-hours
- LLaMA-33B: **90 tCO₂eq**, 233 MWh, 530,432 GPU-hours
- LLaMA-65B: **173 tCO₂eq**, 449 MWh, 1,022,362 GPU-hours
- **Total (all four models, ~5 months): 1,015 tCO₂eq, 2,638 MWh**

For comparison: OPT-175B was 137 tCO₂eq; BLOOM-175B was 183.

**The argument:** by releasing the weights, LLaMA spares many future researchers from re-paying this carbon cost.

**In plain English:** training the 65B alone emitted as much CO₂ as ~50 average Americans emit in a year. The whole LLaMA family was ~300 American-years of emissions. Releasing the weights means others don't have to re-do this.

## Why this paper matters historically

Two distinct legacies:

**1. The architectural recipe.** Pre-norm + RMSNorm + SwiGLU + RoPE became the default. Nearly every open LLM after LLaMA uses these four ingredients. They appear in:
- Mistral 7B, Mixtral 8×7B, Mixtral 8×22B
- Llama 2, Llama 3, Llama 4
- Qwen, Qwen2, Qwen2.5, Qwen3
- Gemma, Gemma 2
- DeepSeek-V2, DeepSeek-V3
- Phi-3, OLMo, MPT, Falcon

When people say "LLaMA-style architecture," this is what they mean.

**2. The conceptual move from compute-optimal to inference-optimal.** Chinchilla optimized one objective; LLaMA reframed the question. Modern small-but-strong models (Mistral 7B, Phi-3, Gemma 2) all lean even harder into "train a small model for a long time" than LLaMA did.

LLaMA also did three less-glamorous-but-important things:
- Demonstrated that *only public data* could reach SOTA quality at scale.
- Released weights that hobbyists could actually run.
- Made transparent the carbon cost of pretraining.

## Limitations

Authors acknowledge:
- No instruction tuning / RLHF — base model only.
- Bias and toxicity scale with model size; not solved.
- TruthfulQA shows substantial hallucination.
- Pretraining mix may underweight books/papers (the MMLU gap).
- Dataset filtering doesn't fully scrub bias-laden web content.

Not stated but worth noting:
- **2048-token context.** Short by modern standards.
- **Multilingual coverage limited to 20 European languages.** No CJK (Chinese, Japanese, Korean), no Arabic, no Hindi.
- **Dense attention only.** No GQA, no sliding window, no MoE — all of which came in subsequent papers.
- **License was research-only at release.** Commercial use waited for Llama 2.

---

## Open Questions

- How much of LLaMA's advantage over Chinchilla is from training duration, vs architecture choices, vs data quality? The paper doesn't ablate.
- Why does "respectful" prompting *hurt* 65B on RealToxicityPrompts? Does scale change how the model interprets framing prompts?
- Marginal contribution of each architectural change (pre-norm vs SwiGLU vs RoPE)? No ablations.
- What's the right tokens-per-parameter ratio for the 7B if you keep training? The paper says it kept improving past 1T — would 5T have been worth it? (Llama 2 partially answered: yes, 2T is better.)

---

## My Takeaways

- The architectural delta in Section 2.2 (pre-norm + RMSNorm + SwiGLU + RoPE) is *the* most influential single section of any 2023 paper. Memorize it.
- Chinchilla showed *compute-optimal*. LLaMA reframed the question to *inference-optimal* — a dramatically more useful objective for production.
- The MMLU gap is a clean signal that **data composition matters**, not just token count. Books and academic papers carry knowledge density that web text doesn't.
- The toxicity-rises-with-scale finding is a quiet warning that subsequent papers (Llama 2 onward) had to address directly with safety training.
- "Public data only" turned out to be a real constraint — but not an insurmountable one. The MMLU gap is real, but not catastrophic.

## Glossary

**Activation function.** A non-linear function applied between linear layers in a neural network. Without it, stacking layers wouldn't add expressive power. Common ones: ReLU, GeLU, Swish, SwiGLU.

**Activation checkpointing.** A memory-saving trick: instead of storing all intermediate activations during the forward pass, recompute them during backward. Trades compute for memory.

**AdamW.** A variant of the Adam optimizer with decoupled weight decay. The de facto default for training Transformers.

**Attention.** The Transformer's mechanism for letting each token attend to other tokens. Computes (Q·K^T)/√d_k, applies softmax, multiplies by V.

**BPE (Byte Pair Encoding).** A subword tokenization algorithm: starting from individual characters, repeatedly merge the most frequent adjacent pair into a single token. Produces a vocabulary that handles out-of-distribution words gracefully.

**Causal mask.** In a decoder-only Transformer, each token can attend only to previous tokens (not future ones). Implemented by setting attention scores for future positions to −∞ before softmax.

**Chinchilla scaling.** Hoffmann 2022's finding that compute-optimal LLM training uses roughly equal scaling of parameters and tokens (~20 tokens per parameter).

**Compute-optimal.** Allocation of parameters and training tokens that minimizes loss for a fixed training-FLOP budget.

**Decoder-only Transformer.** A Transformer using only the autoregressive decoder stack (no encoder). Each token attends only to previous tokens. Standard for modern LLMs.

**FFN (Feedforward Network).** Inside each Transformer block, a position-wise two-layer network applied to each token independently. SwiGLU replaces the activation in this block.

**FlashAttention.** A memory-efficient attention implementation that doesn't materialize the full attention matrix. Used in xformers.

**GPU-hour.** Unit of compute. One A100 running for one hour ≈ 1 GPU-hour. LLaMA-65B used ~1M A100-hours.

**Hidden dimension.** The width of the model — size of the residual stream and most intermediate vectors. LLaMA-65B's hidden dim is 8192.

**Inference.** Using the trained model to generate output. Cost is paid per query, forever.

**Layer.** One Transformer block (attention + FFN + norms + residuals). LLaMA-65B has 80 layers stacked.

**LayerNorm.** A normalization that subtracts the mean and divides by the standard deviation across the feature dim. RMSNorm replaces it in LLaMA.

**Loss.** The training objective. For language models, it's typically cross-entropy on the next-token prediction.

**Model parallelism.** Splitting model parameters across multiple GPUs. Variants: tensor parallelism (split each matrix), pipeline parallelism (split layers), sequence parallelism (split along the sequence dim).

**Parameter.** A learned scalar in the network. A "65B parameter model" has 65 billion of them.

**Pretraining.** Training the model on a large unlabeled corpus to predict the next token. Gives the model its broad knowledge.

**Pre-LN / Pre-normalization.** Applying LayerNorm/RMSNorm to the input of each sub-layer instead of after the residual addition. More stable for deep networks.

**RMSNorm.** LayerNorm minus the mean-centering. Cheaper, equally effective at scale.

**ReLU.** `f(x) = max(0, x)`. The simplest activation. SwiGLU replaces it in LLaMA's FFN.

**RoPE.** Rotary Positional Embedding. Encodes position by rotating Q and K vectors at every attention layer.

**SentencePiece.** Google's BPE/Unigram tokenizer implementation.

**SwiGLU.** Gated activation: `Swish(xW₁) ⊙ (xW₃)`, then output projection. Replaces ReLU in the FFN.

**Swish.** `f(z) = z · σ(z)` where σ is sigmoid. Smooth, non-monotonic activation. Also called SiLU.

**Token.** The unit the model operates on. A subword piece, word, or byte.

**Tokenizer.** Converts text to integer token IDs. LLaMA's vocab size is 32,000.

**Transformer.** Architecture from Vaswani 2017. The basis of all modern LLMs.

**xformers.** Meta's library of memory-efficient attention kernels.

**Zero-shot / Few-shot.** Evaluation modes. Zero-shot: just the question. Few-shot: a few example Q-A pairs before the question.
